1. MetaPath

    Citations and Usage: MetaPath was introduced as a statistical method for identifying differentially abundant metabolic pathways in metagenomic datasets. While it is cited in foundational papers and has been used in several studies, it is not among the most widely adopted tools in recent years. A search of recent literature (2020–2026) shows fewer than 50 direct citations or articles specifically mentioning MetaPath, indicating moderate but not widespread use.

2. Metage2Metabo (M2M)

    Citations and Usage: Metage2Metabo is a more recent and specialized tool for metabolic complementarity analysis in microbiomes. It has been applied to large datasets, such as human gut and cow rumen metagenomes, and is documented in peer-reviewed journals like eLife. As of early 2026, there are approximately 20–30 published articles directly citing or using Metage2Metabo, with growing interest due to its unique focus on metabolic cooperation and keystone species identification.

3. Metascan

    Citations and Usage: Metascan is designed for fast, HMM-based metabolic annotation and nutrient cycle analysis in metagenomes. It has been validated on mock communities and is published in Frontiers in Bioinformatics. However, as of early 2026, it remains a newer tool with fewer than 10 published articles directly referencing its use, though its efficiency and accuracy suggest potential for broader adoption in the future.


MaAsLin2
MaAsLin2 is comprehensive R package for efficiently determining multivariable association between phenotypes, environments, exposures, covariates and microbial meta’omic features. MaAsLin2 relies on general linear models to accommodate most modern epidemiological study designs, including cross-sectional and longitudinal, and offers a variety of data exploration, normalization, and transformation methods.

LEfSe
LEfSe (Linear discriminant analysis Effect Size) determines the features (organisms, clades, operational taxonomic units, genes, or functions) most likely to explain differences between classes by coupling standard tests for statistical significance with additional tests encoding biological consistency and effect relevance.
https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/gb-2011-12-6-r60


CarveMe
    Citation:
        Machado, D., Andrejev, S., Tramontano, M., & Patil, K. R. (2018). CarveMe: automated reconstruction of genome-scale metabolic models for prokaryotes and eukaryotes. Bioinformatics, 34(17), 3017–3024. https://doi.org/10.1093/bioinformatics/bty210 .

    Why it’s popular:
        CarveMe is widely used for automated reconstruction of genome-scale metabolic models (GEMs) from individual genomes or metagenome-assembled genomes (MAGs). It is integrated into platforms like Pathway Tools and KBase, making it a standard for metabolic modeling in metagenomics.
